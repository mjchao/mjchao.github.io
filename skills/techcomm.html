Introduction

I definitely agree that writing code deserves the majority of the effort on a project. At the same time, and perhaps contrary to many people's beliefs, I find that technical communication is a vital aspect of any endeavour and it isn't something one can just pick up and do "properly." In college, I took technical communication classes, and throughout my internships, I applied my skills to producing quality documentation and presentations for my team and managers.

Project Documentation

There are many documents that coding projects require. I've written requirement and design documents. While working as a quality assurance intern at Jump Trading, I generated requirements documentation with software engineers. Afterwards, I created a test plan document and proceeded to build my test framework after it was approved. The project went smoothly and successfully. At Google, I built a component for a machine learning pipeline and towards the end of my internship, I created a thorough document describing the inner workings of my modules and how to maintain them so that they remain in use after I left.

Presentations

I have given many presentations in school and during my internships. I generally receive positive feedback and successfully communicate complicated material in a short period of time. I rehearse frequently and listen to myself to better understand what an audience experiences. I place heavy emphasis on my choice of visuals because I realize that they are more effective than words in a short span of time. In the past, I have managed to explain complicated algorithms, such as noise constrastive estimation loss for word vectors, in ten minutes. I've also given effective end-of-internship project presentations.
